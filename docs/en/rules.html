Game Basics

Ability Scores & Modifiers Category: Basics
The six abilities and how modifiers work

Every creature has six ability scores: Strength (STR), Dexterity (DEX), Constitution (CON), Intelligence (INT), Wisdom (WIS), Charisma (CHA). Scores normally range from 1 to 20 (20 is the cap for ordinary creatures).

Modifier:
(Score − 10) / 2, rounded down

The modifier is added to almost every roll: attacks, checks, and saving throws. For example, a score of 14 gives a +2 modifier, while 8 gives −1.

Advantage & Disadvantage Category: Basics
Rolling dice in favorable or unfavorable conditions

Sometimes a feature or spell grants advantage or disadvantage on an attack roll, saving throw, or ability check.

  • Advantage: roll two d20s and take the higher result.
  • Disadvantage: roll two d20s and take the lower result.

They don't stack: no matter how many sources apply, you roll just one extra die. If you have both advantage and disadvantage, they cancel out and you roll a single normal d20.

Ability Checks & Skills Category: Basics
How to make skill checks

When the outcome of an action is uncertain, the DM calls for an ability check. Each skill is tied to one of the six abilities.

Formula:
d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus (if proficient in the skill)

Difficulty Class (DC): very easy 5, easy 10, medium 15, hard 20, very hard 25, nearly impossible 30.

Saving Throws Category: Basics
Resisting threats and effects

A saving throw is an attempt to resist a threat: a spell, a trap, a poison, or a disease. It's usually forced on you by an effect rather than something you choose.

d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus (if proficient in that save)

The DC is set by the source of the effect (for example, a caster's spell save DC). Saving throw proficiencies come from your class and certain features.

Proficiency Bonus Category: Basics
What proficiency adds

Your proficiency bonus is based on character level, not class, and is added to anything you're proficient with: skill checks, attack rolls, and saving throws.

  • Levels 1–4: +2
  • Levels 5–8: +3
  • Levels 9–12: +4
  • Levels 13–16: +5
  • Levels 17–20: +6

You add your proficiency bonus to a roll only once (except when expertise doubles it).

Passive Checks Category: Basics
Checks made without rolling

A passive check is a check made without rolling, useful for routine tasks or for secretly determining whether characters notice something.

10 + all modifiers that normally apply to the check

Add +5 for advantage, −5 for disadvantage. The most common is passive Perception, compared against a Stealth roll to notice hidden creatures.

Heroic Inspiration Category: Basics
A reward for good play

In the 2024 rules this is called Heroic Inspiration. The DM grants it for roleplaying, cleverness, or heroics. You either have it or you don't — it doesn't stack.

Spend Heroic Inspiration to reroll any d20 and use the new result. Some species (such as humans) grant it automatically.

Combat & Actions

The Combat Round & Turn Category: Combat
What you can do on a single turn

Combat runs in rounds of 6 seconds. On your turn you can usually:

  • Move up to your speed.
  • Take one action.
  • Possibly one bonus action (if something grants it).

Outside your turn you may take one reaction per round. Movement can be split before and after your action.

Initiative Category: Combat
Who acts first

At the start of combat everyone makes a Dexterity (initiative) check. Participants act in order from highest to lowest result.

This order holds for the rest of the fight. Ties are broken by the higher Dexterity modifier or by agreement among the players.

Actions in Combat Category: Combat
The full list of standard actions

On your turn you can choose one of these actions:

  • Attack — make a melee or ranged attack.
  • Cast a Spell — cast a spell with a casting time of 1 action.
  • Dash — double your speed for the turn.
  • Disengage — your movement doesn't provoke opportunity attacks.
  • Dodge — attacks against you have disadvantage; you make Dexterity saves with advantage.
  • Help — give an ally advantage.
  • Hide — make a Stealth check.
  • Ready — prepare an action with a trigger.
  • Search — look for something.
  • Use an Object — interact with an object.
Bonus Action Category: Combat
An extra, quick action

A bonus action can't be taken freely — a specific feature, spell, or ability grants it (for example, two-weapon fighting or a rogue's Cunning Action).

You can take only one bonus action per turn, and you choose when to take it during your turn.

Reaction Category: Combat
An instant response to a trigger

A reaction is an instant action taken in response to a trigger, possible even when it isn't your turn. The most common example is the opportunity attack.

You have one reaction per round; it refreshes at the start of your next turn.

Opportunity Attacks Category: Combat
Striking a fleeing enemy

When a hostile creature leaves your reach by moving away, you can spend your reaction to make one melee attack against it.

It can be avoided with the Disengage action, by teleporting, or by being moved without using its own movement.

Movement & Speed Category: Combat
How movement works

You can move up to your speed and split it before and after your action — you can even move between attacks.

  • Difficult terrain costs double movement.
  • Standing up from prone costs half your speed.
  • You can move freely through an ally's space, but not an enemy's.
Making an Attack Category: Combat
How to hit a target

To hit, roll a d20 and compare it to the target's Armor Class (AC).

d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus (if proficient with the weapon)

Melee usually uses Strength, ranged uses Dexterity. A finesse weapon lets you pick the better of the two. A total ≥ AC is a hit.

Armor Class (AC) Category: Combat
How hard you are to hit

Armor Class measures how hard a creature is to hit. An attack hits if its total equals or exceeds the AC.

With no armor, AC = 10 + Dexterity modifier. Armor sets a base (sometimes limiting Dexterity), and a shield usually adds +2.

Critical Hits Category: Combat
What a natural 20 does

If you roll a natural 20 on an attack roll, it's a critical hit — it hits regardless of AC.

On a crit you roll double the damage dice (modifiers are added once). A natural 1 on an attack is an automatic miss.

Cover Category: Combat
Protection from obstacles

Obstacles between attacker and target grant cover:

  • Half cover (low wall): +2 to AC and Dexterity saves.
  • Three-quarters cover (arrow slit): +5 to AC and Dexterity saves.
  • Total cover: the target can't be targeted directly.

Only the most protective degree applies; cover doesn't stack.

Damage & Damage Types Category: Combat
Resistance, vulnerability, immunity

Damage is rolled from a weapon or spell's dice plus modifiers. The damage type (fire, cold, piercing, etc.) interacts with the target's defenses:

  • Resistance: damage is halved.
  • Vulnerability: damage is doubled.
  • Immunity: no damage is taken.

Apply all additions and subtractions first, and only then resistance or vulnerability.

Combat Maneuvers

Two-Weapon Fighting Category: Maneuvers
Attacking with a weapon in each hand

If you attack with a light melee weapon in one hand, you can use a bonus action to attack with a different light weapon in your other hand.

You don't add your ability modifier to the bonus attack's damage (unless it's negative) without a relevant feature or fighting style.

Unarmed Strike Category: Maneuvers
Fighting without a weapon

An unarmed strike (fist, elbow, knee, etc.) is a melee attack. In the 2024 rules you choose one option instead of an attack roll:

  • Damage: a hit deals 1 + Strength modifier bludgeoning damage.
  • Grapple: impose the Grappled condition (target saves).
  • Shove: knock prone or push 5 feet (target saves).

Classes such as the monk greatly improve unarmed strikes.

Grappling Category: Maneuvers
How to grab a target

In the 2024 rules, grappling is an Unarmed Strike option, not a contest. You declare a Grapple; the target (no more than one size larger) makes a Strength or Dexterity saving throw (its choice) against DC = 8 + your Strength modifier + proficiency bonus.

On a failure the target is Grappled: its speed is 0. It can escape as an action with a Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics) check against that same DC.

Shoving Category: Maneuvers
Knock prone or push away

Shoving is also an Unarmed Strike option. The target makes a Strength or Dexterity saving throw against DC = 8 + your Strength modifier + proficiency bonus.

On a failure you either knock it prone (the Prone condition) or push it 5 feet away.

Readying an Action (Ready) Category: Maneuvers
Acting on a chosen trigger

With the Ready action you choose a trigger and a response, for example: “If an enemy steps through the door, I shoot.”

When the trigger occurs, you take the readied action as a reaction. A readied spell requires concentration while you wait.

Ranged Attacks in Combat Category: Maneuvers
Range and shooting up close

A ranged weapon has a normal and a long range (e.g., 80/320). Beyond normal range you attack with disadvantage; beyond long range you can't attack at all.

If an enemy is within 5 feet of you, ranged attacks are also made with disadvantage.

Mounted Combat Category: Maneuvers
Fighting from a mount

Mounting or dismounting costs half your speed. A mount acts on your initiative.

You can control a trained mount (limiting it to the Dash, Disengage, and Dodge actions) or ride an independent mount that acts on its own.

Improvised Weapons Category: Maneuvers
Fighting with whatever is at hand

A chair, a bottle, or a rock — almost anything can be a weapon. An improvised weapon usually deals 1d4 damage.

If an object resembles a real weapon, the DM may let it count as that weapon instead.

Magic & Spells

Spellcasting Basics Category: Magic
What makes up a spell

Every spell has a few key parameters:

  • Level (0 for cantrips, up to 9).
  • Casting time (action, bonus action, reaction, minutes).
  • Range, components, and duration.

Casting a spell that takes an action uses the Cast a Spell action in combat.

Spell Slots Category: Magic
The fuel for magic

To cast a 1st-level or higher spell, you expend a slot of that level or higher. Slots are regained on a rest (usually a long rest).

Casting a spell using a higher-level slot often strengthens it (more damage or targets), if the spell's description says so.

Cantrips Category: Magic
Level 0 spells

Cantrips are level 0 spells you know by heart. They don't expend slots and can be cast at will.

Many damaging cantrips (for example, Fire Bolt) scale up at character levels 5, 11, and 17.

Preparing & Knowing Spells Category: Magic
Two ways to have spells

In the 2024 rules, almost all full casters prepare spells from their class list. The number prepared grows with level, and you can change the list as you level up.

The wizard still keeps a spellbook and prepares a subset from it. The old 'known spells' concept is mostly gone — replaced by a fixed prepared list.

Components (V, S, M) Category: Magic
What a spell requires to cast

Most spells require components:

  • Verbal (V): spoken words — fails if you can't speak.
  • Somatic (S): gestures — you need a free hand.
  • Material (M): an item; a focus or component pouch replaces it unless a cost is listed.
Concentration Category: Magic
Maintaining ongoing spells

Some spells require concentration to keep going. You can concentrate on only one such spell at a time.

Concentration breaks if you: cast another concentration spell, become incapacitated or die, or fail a Constitution save after taking damage.

Save DC = 10 or half the damage taken, whichever is higher
Rituals Category: Magic
Casting without a slot

A spell with the ritual tag can be cast by adding 10 minutes to its casting time without expending a slot.

This works only out of combat and only if your class supports ritual casting.

Spell Attack & Save DC Category: Magic
Hitting and forcing saves

Depending on the spell, you either make a spell attack or force the target to make a saving throw.

Spell attack: d20 + proficiency bonus + spellcasting ability modifier
Spell save DC: 8 + proficiency bonus + spellcasting ability modifier

Your spellcasting ability depends on your class (INT, WIS, or CHA).

Schools of Magic Category: Magic
The eight spell traditions

Every spell belongs to one of eight schools: Abjuration, Conjuration, Divination, Enchantment, Evocation, Illusion, Necromancy, Transmutation.

The school is mostly descriptive, but some features and items interact with specific schools.

Conditions

Blinded Category: Conditions
The creature can't see
  • The creature can't see and automatically fails any check requiring sight.
  • Attacks against it have advantage; its own attacks have disadvantage.
Charmed Category: Conditions
Can't harm the charmer
  • The creature can't attack the charmer or target it with harmful effects.
  • The charmer has advantage on social checks to interact with it.
Deafened Category: Conditions
The creature can't hear
  • The creature can't hear and automatically fails any check requiring hearing.
Frightened Category: Conditions
Fear of a source
  • Disadvantage on checks and attacks while the source of fear is in line of sight.
  • The creature can't willingly move closer to the source of its fear.
Grappled Category: Conditions
Speed becomes 0
  • The creature's speed becomes 0 and can't benefit from any bonus to speed.
  • It ends if the grappler is incapacitated or an effect moves them apart.
Incapacitated Category: Conditions
No actions or reactions
  • The creature can take no actions and no reactions.

Many stronger conditions (stunned, paralyzed) also make a creature incapacitated.

Invisible Category: Conditions
The creature can't be seen
  • The creature can't be seen without special senses and is heavily obscured.
  • Attacks against it have disadvantage; its attacks have advantage.
Paralyzed Category: Conditions
Can't move or speak
  • The creature is incapacitated, can't move or speak, and fails Strength and Dexterity saves.
  • Attacks against it have advantage; any hit from within 5 feet is a critical hit.
Petrified Category: Conditions
Turned to stone
  • The creature is transformed into solid substance, incapacitated and unaware of its surroundings.
  • It has resistance to all damage and immunity to poison and disease; attacks against it have advantage.
Poisoned Category: Conditions
Disadvantage on attacks and checks
  • The creature has disadvantage on attack rolls and ability checks.
Prone Category: Conditions
The creature is lying down
  • It can only crawl (or stand up, spending half its speed).
  • Its own attacks have disadvantage. Attacks against it: advantage from within 5 feet, disadvantage from farther away.
Restrained Category: Conditions
Speed 0 and disadvantage
  • Speed becomes 0.
  • Attacks against it have advantage, its attacks have disadvantage; disadvantage on Dexterity saves.
Stunned Category: Conditions
A nearly helpless creature
  • The creature is incapacitated, can't move, can speak only falteringly, and fails Strength and Dexterity saves.
  • Attacks against it have advantage.
Unconscious Category: Conditions
The creature is knocked out
  • Incapacitated, unaware of surroundings, drops what it's holding and falls prone.
  • Fails Strength and Dexterity saves; attacks against it have advantage, and hits from within 5 feet are critical.
Exhaustion Category: Conditions
The exhaustion track (2024 rules)

Exhaustion has 6 levels. Each level adds a cumulative penalty:

  • −2 per level to all d20 rolls (attacks, checks, saves).
  • −5 feet × level to speed.

At level 6 the creature dies. Each long rest removes 1 level.

Rest & Health

Hit Points (HP) Category: Rest
How much damage you can take

Hit points measure your durability. Damage is subtracted from current HP. At 0 HP you fall unconscious (for player characters, death saving throws begin).

HP can't drop below 0; excess damage is ignored, except for the instant-death rule from a single massive blow.

Hit Dice Category: Rest
Your personal healing resource

You have a number of Hit Dice equal to your level (the type depends on your class, e.g. d8). During a short rest you can spend some of them to recover HP (roll + Constitution modifier).

A long rest restores half of your spent Hit Dice.

Short Rest Category: Rest
An hour of downtime

A short rest is at least 1 hour of light activity. During it you can spend Hit Dice to heal.

Some class features (such as a warlock's spell slots) recharge specifically on a short rest.

Long Rest Category: Rest
A full rest

A long rest is at least 8 hours (sleep or light activity). You regain all HP, half your spent Hit Dice, and most expended resources (slots, etc.).

You can benefit from a long rest only once per 24 hours.

Death Saving Throws Category: Rest
Fighting for life at 0 HP

At 0 HP, at the start of your turn you roll a d20 (no modifiers):

  • 10+ is a success, 9 or less is a failure.
  • Three successes — you're stable; three failures — you die.
  • 20 — you regain 1 HP; 1 — counts as two failures.

Taking damage at 0 HP adds a failure (a hit from within 5 feet counts as two).

Stabilizing a Creature Category: Rest
Saving a dying creature

To stabilize a creature at 0 HP, take an action to make a Medicine check (DC 10) or use a relevant ability.

A stable creature stops making death saves, stays unconscious, and regains 1 HP after 1d4 hours (or immediately from any healing).

Temporary Hit Points Category: Rest
A temporary buffer

Temporary HP are a buffer on top of normal HP: damage depletes them first. They aren't healing and don't stack (take the larger source).

They go away on a long rest or when the granting effect ends.

Healing Category: Rest
Recovering hit points

Healing (from spells, potions, or rest) restores HP but never above your maximum. Any healing at 0 HP brings a creature back up and conscious.

Travel & Environment

Travel Pace Category: Environment
Fast, normal, slow

While travelling, the party picks a pace:

  • Fast: +1 mile/hour, but −5 to passive Perception.
  • Normal: standard speed.
  • Slow: slower, but you can move stealthily.

Roughly: ~3–4 miles per hour, ~24–30 miles per day at a normal pace.

Vision & Light Category: Environment
Bright, dim, darkness
  • Bright light: you see normally.
  • Dim light — a lightly obscured area: disadvantage on sight-based Perception checks.
  • Darkness — a heavily obscured area: effectively the Blinded condition.

Darkvision lets you see in darkness as if it were dim light, out to a set range.

Hiding & Stealth Category: Environment
How to hide from enemies

In the 2024 rules, the Hide action requires a Dexterity (Stealth) check against DC 15 while out of line of sight (in heavy obscurity or behind full cover).

On a success you gain the Invisible condition: enemies don't know your location. It ends when you attack, cast a spell with a verbal component, make noise, or are found.

Falling Category: Environment
Damage from heights

A fall deals 1d6 bludgeoning damage per 10 feet fallen, to a maximum of 20d6 (at 200 feet or more).

A creature that falls lands Prone, unless the fall was somehow controlled.

Suffocation Category: Environment
When there's no air

A creature can hold its breath for 1 + its Constitution modifier minutes (minimum 30 seconds).

When it runs out, it can survive for a number of rounds equal to its Constitution modifier (minimum 1), then drops to 0 HP and is dying.

Difficult Terrain Category: Environment
Movement costs double

Rubble, dense forest, deep snow, stairs, and the like are difficult terrain. Each foot of movement there costs 2 feet.

Difficult terrain doesn't stack: it's either present or not.

Climbing, Swimming, Jumping Category: Environment
Special movement
  • Climbing and swimming: each foot costs an extra foot of movement (like difficult terrain).
  • Long jump: with a running start, a number of feet equal to your Strength score.
  • High jump: with a running start, 3 + your Strength modifier feet.
Carrying Capacity Category: Environment
How much you can carry

Your carrying capacity equals your Strength score × 15 pounds. Exceeding it slows and encumbers you.

You can push, drag, or lift up to twice your carrying capacity, but your speed drops to 5 feet while doing so.

Equipment & Wealth

Currency Category: Equipment
Coins and exchange rates

The standard coin is the gold piece (gp). Exchange rates:

  • 10 copper (cp) = 1 silver (sp)
  • 10 silver = 1 gold (gp)
  • 10 gold = 1 platinum (pp)

The electrum piece (ep) equals 5 silver, but it's uncommon.

Armor & Shields Category: Equipment
How armor affects AC
  • Light: AC = base + your full Dexterity modifier.
  • Medium: AC = base + Dexterity (max +2).
  • Heavy: fixed AC, no Dexterity; may require a Strength score.
  • Shield: usually +2 to AC.

Some armor imposes disadvantage on Stealth. Wearing armor you lack proficiency with hampers attacks and spellcasting.

Weapon Properties Category: Equipment
Key weapon tags

Weapons have property tags that change how they are used:

  • Finesse: use Strength or Dexterity, your choice.
  • Light: suitable for two-weapon fighting.
  • Heavy: small creatures have disadvantage with it.
  • Two-Handed: needs both hands.
  • Versatile: more damage when wielded in two hands.
  • Reach: +5 feet of reach.
  • Thrown: can be thrown.
  • Ammunition: needs arrows or bolts.
Buying & Selling Category: Equipment
Trading gear

Gear is bought at list prices. Selling property usually fetches about half its value.

Trade goods (metals, salt, livestock) keep full value and often serve for large deals instead of coins.

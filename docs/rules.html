Game Basics

Advantage & Disadvantage Category: Basics
How to roll dice in favorable or unfavorable conditions

Sometimes a special ability or spell tells you that you have advantage or disadvantage on an attack roll, saving throw, or ability check.

  • Advantage: You roll a second d20 and use the higher of the two results.
  • Disadvantage: You roll a second d20 and use the lower of the two results.

Important rule: If you have both advantage and disadvantage on the same roll, they cancel each other out. Even if you have two sources of advantage and one of disadvantage, you still make a normal roll (rolling one d20).

Ability Checks (Skills) Category: Basics
How to make skill checks outside of combat

When you try to do something that has a chance of failure, the Dungeon Master (DM) asks for an ability check. Each skill is tied to one of the 6 core abilities.

Roll formula:
d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus (if proficient in the skill)

Difficulty (DC): The DM sets the Difficulty Class. For example: Easy (10), Medium (15), Hard (20), Very Hard (25), Nearly Impossible (30).

Combat & Actions

Turn Structure in Combat Category: Combat
What you can do on a single turn

Combat is divided into rounds (1 round = 6 seconds of game-world time). On your turn you have the following at your disposal:

  • Movement: You can move up to your speed. Movement can be split into parts before and after your action.
  • Action: The main thing you do. Attack, cast a spell, dash, help, and so on.
  • Bonus Action: An extra quick action. You can take it only when, you have a class feature, spell, or trait that explicitly says it's a bonus action (for example, drinking a potion, the "Healing Word" spell).
  • Reaction: Taken not on your turn, but in response to a certain event (for example, an opportunity attack, the "Shield" spell). You have only 1 reaction per round.
  • Free Object Interaction: Draw a sword, open a door, pick up an item. One such interaction per turn is free.
Types of Actions in Combat Category: Combat
Attack, Dash, Disengage, Hide, Help, and others

When your turn comes, you can spend your Action on one of the following:

  • Attack: Strike with a melee or ranged weapon. Some classes (Fighter) allow multiple strikes with one Attack action.
  • Cast a Spell: Cast a spell whose casting time is "1 action".
  • Dash: Your movement speed is doubled for this turn.
  • Disengage: Until the end of your turn, your movement doesn't provoke opportunity attacks from enemies.
  • Dodge: Until your next turn, any attacks against you are made with disadvantage, and you have advantage on Dexterity saving throws.
  • Help: You give an ally advantage on their next ability check or attack roll against an enemy next to you.
  • Hide: You make a Dexterity (Stealth) check to hide from enemies who can't see you.
  • Ready: You delay your action until a certain condition occurs (for example: "If the goblin comes around the corner, I shoot"). When the condition is met, you use your Reactionto perform the action.
Opportunity Attack Category: Combat
How and when you can strike a fleeing enemy

You can rarely walk calmly past an enemy without consequences. If an enemy you can see moves out of your melee reach (usually more than 5 feet), it provokes an opportunity attack from you.

How it works: You use your Reactionto make one melee weapon attack against this enemy. The attack happens right before it leaves your reach.

Exceptions: You can't make this attack if the enemy used the "Disengage" action, or if someone moved it with magic or pushed it against its will.

Magic & Spells

Magic & Concentration Category: Magic
Rules for maintaining magical effects

Some spells require concentration to maintain their effect. This means you need to focus on the magic. You can end concentration at any time (it doesn't require an action).

  • One spell: You can't concentrate on two spells at once. If you cast a new spell with concentration, the old one instantly ends.
  • Taking damage: If you take damage while concentrating, you must make a Constitution saving throw. The DC equals 10 or half the damage taken, whichever is higher.
  • Losing consciousness: If you drop to 0 hit points or are incapacitated, concentration is automatically broken.
Spell Components (V, S, M) Category: Magic
What's needed to cast spells

Each spell requires certain components to cast:

  • Verbal (V): Specific mystic words. You can't cast if you're gagged, underwater (without being able to breathe), or in the area of a "Silence" spell.
  • Somatic (S): Complex hand gestures. You must have at least one free hand to perform the gestures.
  • Material (M): Physical items. If a spell doesn't list a component cost (e.g. "a feather"), you can use a Component Pouch or an Arcane Focus instead. If a cost is listed (e.g. "a 50 gp diamond"), you need that specific item. The hand holding the focus/material can also perform somatic gestures.

Conditions

Blinded, Deafened, Invisible Category: Conditions
Consequences of losing senses or sight
  • Blinded: A creature can't see and automatically fails any check that requires sight. Its attacks are made with disadvantage. Attacks by other creatures against it are made with advantage.
  • Deafened: A creature can't hear and automatically fails checks that rely on hearing.
  • Invisible: A creature is invisible (though it can be detected by sounds and tracks). The invisible creature's attacks are made with advantage, and attacks against it with disadvantage.
Prone Category: Conditions
What happens when you're lying on the ground

When you're knocked down or fall voluntarily, you become "Prone".

  • Your only movement option is to crawl (every 1 foot of movement costs 2 feet).
  • To stand up, you need to spend half your maximum speed (for example, 15 feet if your speed is 30).
  • You have disadvantage on all your own attack rolls.
  • Attacks against you in melee (within 5 feet) are made with advantage.
  • Attacks against you from afar (arrows, ranged magic) are made with disadvantage.
Charmed & Frightened Category: Conditions
Psychological effects of magic and monsters
  • Charmed: A charmed creature can't attack the charmer or target it with harmful magical effects. The charmer has advantage on any social checks against this creature.
  • Frightened: A frightened creature has disadvantage on ability checks and attack rolls while the source of its fear is within its line of sight. In addition, the creature can't willingly move closer to the source of its fear.
Restrained, Stunned, Paralyzed Category: Conditions
The most dangerous effects in the game
  • Restrained: Speed becomes 0. Your attacks have disadvantage. Attacks against you have advantage. You have disadvantage on Dexterity saving throws.
  • Stunned: You're incapacitated (can't take actions/reactions). Your speed is 0 and you can only mumble. You automatically fail Strength and Dexterity saving throws. Attacks against you have advantage.
  • Paralyzed: The same as Stunned, BUT: any attack that hits you in melee (within 5 feet) automatically becomes a critical hit. This is deadly dangerous!

Rest & Health

Short & Long Rest Category: Rest
How to recover between battles
  • Short Rest: Lasts at least 1 hour without strenuous activity (eating, reading, bandaging). At the end you can spend your Hit Diceto heal. Some class abilities also recharge (such as Warlock magic or a Monk's Ki).
  • Long Rest: Lasts 8 hours, of which at least 6 hours are sleep and no more than 2 hours of light activity (keeping watch). At the end you fully restore all your HP, all spell slots, and recover spent Hit Dice equal to half your level.

A character can't benefit from more than one Long Rest in a 24-hour period. To rest, you must have at least 1 hit point.

Death & Death Saving Throws Category: Rest
What happens when hit points drop to zero

If your hit points (HP) drop to 0, you don't die immediately, but fall unconscious and drop to the ground.

At the start of each of your turns you must make a Death Saving Throw. You roll 1d20 without any modifiers:

  • 10 or higher: This is a success. If you accumulate 3 successes, you stabilize and no longer die (but remain unconscious at 0 HP).
  • 9 or lower: This is a failure. If you accumulate 3 failures, your character dies permanently.
  • Roll a 1: This counts as TWO failures.
  • Roll a 20: You instantly regain consciousness and gain 1 HP. You can keep fighting!

Taking damage: If you're hit while at 0 HP, you automatically gain 1 failure (or 2 failures if the enemy hit you in melee, which counts as a critical hit). If any healing restores at least 1 HP to you, all your successes and failures are erased and you wake up.

Combat: Maneuvers

Unarmed Strike (2024) Category: Combat: Maneuvers
Three options: Damage, Grapple, or Shove

In the 2024 edition, an unarmed strike isn't just damage. Instead of a weapon attack you can make an unarmed strike and choose one from three options:

  • Damage: 1 + your Strength modifier bludgeoning damage.
  • Grapple: grab the target (see below).
  • Shove: push the target (see below).
Grapple (2024) Category: Combat: Maneuvers
How to grab an opponent with an unarmed strike

By choosing the Grappleoption, you try to grab the target (no more than one size larger than you and within reach).

Target's save:
Strength or Dexterity saving throw (target's choice) against DC = 8 + your Str mod + proficiency bonus

On a failure, the target gains the Grappledcondition: its speed becomes 0. The grapple ends if you're incapacitated or the target moves out of your reach (for example, it's pushed away).

Shove (2024) Category: Combat: Maneuvers
Knock prone or push 5 feet away

By choosing the Shoveoption, you push a target within reach (no more than one size larger than you).

Target's save:
Strength or Dexterity saving throw against DC = 8 + your Str mod + proficiency bonus

On a failure, you either knock the target prone (Prone condition), or push it 5 feet away from you (your choice).

Two-Weapon Fighting Category: Combat: Maneuvers
An extra attack with a second light weapon

If you take the Attack action with a melee weapon that has the Lightproperty, you can as a bonus action attack with another light weapon you're holding in your other hand.

  • You don't add your ability modifier to the damage of this bonus attack (unless it's negative).
  • The "Two-Weapon Fighting" fighting style lets you add the modifier to this attack as well.
Critical Hit Category: Combat: Maneuvers
What happens on a roll of "20" to attack

When an attack roll comes up as a natural 20 (the exact number on the die, before modifiers), it's a critical hit: the attack automatically hits regardless of AC.

  • You roll all damage dice twice and add them together, then add the modifiers.
  • Extra damage dice (for example, from Sneak Attack) are also doubled.
  • In the 2024 edition, only attack rolls can crit or critically "miss" attacks checks and saving throws don't give a crit.
Surprise (2024) Category: Combat: Maneuvers
How a surprise attack works in the new edition

In the 2024 edition, "surprise" works more simply. If a creature didn't notice a threat at the start of combat (for example, it lost Stealth against Perception), it doesn't lose its turnas before.

Instead, such a creature gets disadvantage on its initiative roll. This makes a prepared ambush valuable, but not as devastating.

Travel & Environment

Cover Category: Environment
AC bonuses from cover

Obstacles between the attacker and the target provide cover. The most advantageous one is used:

  • Half (1/2): +2 to AC and Dexterity saving throws (a low wall, furniture, another creature).
  • Three-quarters (3/4): +5 to AC and Dexterity saving throws (an arrow slit, a tree trunk).
  • Full: the target can't be attacked directly or targeted by a spell.
Lighting & Visibility Category: Environment
Bright, dim light, and darkness

Lighting affects how well you can see:

  • Bright light: normal visibility.
  • Dim light (lightly obscured): disadvantage on Perception checks that rely on sight.
  • Darkness (heavily obscured): the area is effectively blind, creatures have the Blinded condition regarding targets in it.

Darkvision: lets you see in darkness as if it were dim light within its range (usually 60 or 120 feet), but only in shades of gray.

Difficult Terrain Category: Environment
Why movement is slowed by half

Rubble, dense forest, snow, mud, stairs in a crowd, and so on, this is difficult terrain. Each foot of movement through it costs 2 feet of movement.

So at a speed of 30 feet you'll move only 15 feet through difficult terrain. Difficult terrain effects don't stack with each other.

Jumping Category: Environment
Long jump and high jump formulas
  • Long jump: with a running start (min. 10 feet) you cover a distance in feet equal to your Strength score. Without a running start, half as much.
  • High jump: with a running start you jump up 3 + your Strength modifier feet. Without a running start, half as much.

Each foot of a jump costs a foot of movement.

Falling Category: Environment
Damage from falling from a height

A fall deals 1d6 bludgeoning damage per 10 feet of falling, maximum 20d6 (that is, from 200 feet and above).

A creature that has fallen ends up in the Pronecondition, unless it avoided the damage entirely (for example, with the Feather Fall spell).

Holding Breath & Suffocation Category: Environment
How long you can last without air

A creature can hold its breath for 1 + your Constitution modifier minutes (minimum 30 seconds).

When the air runs out, the creature can last a number of additional rounds equal to its Constitution modifier (minimum 1). At the start of its next turn it drops to 0 hit points and starts dying, and can't regain hit points until it can breathe.

Carrying Capacity Category: Environment
How much weight you can carry

Your carrying capacity in pounds = Strength score × 15. Within these limits, the weight of equipment usually doesn't hinder you.

You can drag, lift, or push twice as much weight (Strength × 30), but moving it turns the space into difficult terrain. Small creatures carry the same amount, but large ones carry more.

Conditions: Advanced

Grappled Category: Conditions
Condition from a successful grapple
  • The creature's speed becomes 0, it can't benefit from any bonus to speed.
  • Disadvantage on attack rolls against anyone other than the one who grappled it.
  • The condition ends if the grappler is incapacitated or they're separated.
Incapacitated Category: Conditions
Can't act or react
  • A creature can't take actions, bonus actions, or reactions.
  • It can't speak or concentrate on spells.
  • This condition is often a part of others (Stunned, Paralyzed, Unconscious).
Poisoned Category: Conditions
A common negative condition

A creature with the Poisoned condition has disadvantage on all attack rolls and ability checks. Many poisons and diseases impose this condition for a certain time.

Petrified Category: Conditions
Turning to stone
  • The creature and everything it's carrying turns into a solid substance (stone): weight ×10, it stops aging.
  • It's incapacitated, unaware of its surroundings, and can't move or speak.
  • Resistance to all damage, immunity to poison and disease, automatic failure of Strength and Dexterity saving throws.
Unconscious Category: Conditions
Loss of consciousness
  • A creature is incapacitated, unaware of its surroundings, drops what it's holding, and falls (Prone condition).
  • Automatic failure of Strength and Dexterity saving throws.
  • Attacks against it have advantage, and any hit from close range is a critical.
Exhaustion (2024) Category: Conditions
The updated exhaustion scale

In the 2024 edition, exhaustion became simpler and has 6 levels. For each level:

  • −2 per level to all d20 rolls (attacks, checks, saves).
  • −5 feet × level to speed.

At level 6 the creature dies. Each long rest removes 1 level of exhaustion.
